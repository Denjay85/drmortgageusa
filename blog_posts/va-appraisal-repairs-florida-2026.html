VA Appraisal Repairs in Florida: What Veterans Should Know Before Closing

By Dennis Ross, NMLS #2018381 | June 8, 2026

A VA appraisal is not a home inspection. That is the first thing veterans need to understand.

The VA appraisal helps confirm value and checks for minimum property requirements. It is not designed to find every issue in the house. A separate home inspection is still one of the smartest tools a buyer can use.

In Florida, property condition, insurance, roof age, repairs, and appraisal timing can all affect a VA purchase.

What the VA Appraiser Is Looking For

VA minimum property requirements focus on whether the home is safe, sound, sanitary, and suitable for occupancy. The appraiser is not trying to make the house perfect. They are looking for issues that may affect livability, safety, or the lender's ability to close the loan.

  • Major safety hazards
  • Peeling paint in older homes when lead-based paint rules may apply
  • Broken windows or missing handrails where safety is an issue
  • Roof problems that affect remaining useful life
  • Non-functioning utilities or major system concerns
  • Wood-destroying organism concerns when required
  • Water intrusion, structural concerns, or obvious health hazards

Repairs Can Delay Closing

If the VA appraiser calls out required repairs, those items usually need to be completed and re-inspected before closing. That can create a timing issue if the seller is slow, the contractor is backed up, or the repair is more complicated than expected.

This is why VA buyers should talk through property condition before the offer is written, not after the appraisal comes back.

Who Pays for VA Repairs?

The seller, buyer, agents, or another permitted party may be involved depending on the contract, loan rules, and what the lender allows. The cleanest structure depends on the specific repair and the deal.

Do not assume every repair can be handled the same way. Before money changes hands or repair agreements are made, the lender should be involved so the fix does not create a compliance problem.

Do Not Skip the Home Inspection

A VA appraisal is not enough protection by itself. The appraiser may not test every outlet, climb every roof, inspect every pipe, or uncover every hidden issue.

A home inspection helps you understand what you are buying. It also helps separate small maintenance items from repairs that could affect safety, insurance, financing, or your budget after closing.

Florida Insurance Still Matters

Even if a repair item does not stop the VA loan by itself, it may affect insurance. Roof age, electrical systems, plumbing, prior damage, or condition issues can create insurance friction.

For Florida veterans, the VA conversation and insurance conversation should happen together. A property that technically meets one standard can still create a payment or coverage problem if insurance is expensive or hard to bind.

The Bottom Line

VA loans are powerful, but the property still has to work.

Before you write the offer, look at condition, repairs, appraisal risk, inspection strategy, insurance, and timing. The goal is not just getting a VA offer accepted. The goal is getting it closed without last-minute surprises.
